Appendix: Additional Reading

Where to go next on the stack and the ideas the pattern leans on, from the official docs to the people who named these patterns.

The Stack

Official Documentation

Start here when you want the authoritative version of anything we touched.

.NET Dependency Injection

The official guide to Microsoft.Extensions.DependencyInjection: registering services, the three lifetimes, and how the container resolves a graph. Read it to understand exactly what the composition root chapter set up.

https://learn.microsoft.com/dotnet/core/extensions/dependency-injection

Service Lifetimes

A focused page on singleton, scoped, and transient, including the captive-dependency pitfall where a long-lived object accidentally traps a short-lived one. Directly relevant to why we registered everything as scoped.

https://learn.microsoft.com/dotnet/core/extensions/dependency-injection#service-lifetimes

Entity Framework Core

The home for the ORM behind the persistence layer: the DbContext, DbSet, model configuration, and the full list of database providers you can swap in at the composition root.

https://learn.microsoft.com/ef/core/

System.Text.Json

The serializer the import service uses, including JsonSerializerOptions, the [JsonPropertyName] attribute, and case-insensitive matching. Good background for the parsing decisions in the Import Service chapter.

https://learn.microsoft.com/dotnet/standard/serialization/system-text-json/overview

The Ideas

Patterns and Principles

The names and reasoning behind the moves we made, straight from the people who articulated them.

The Repository Pattern

Martin Fowler's catalog entry for the pattern that gave us the thin wall over the database. Useful for seeing the original framing and where a repository ends and a query object begins.

https://martinfowler.com/eaaCatalog/repository.html

Inversion of Control and Dependency Injection

The essay that popularized the distinction between depending on abstractions and constructing them yourself. It explains why the composition root exists and why classes accept their collaborators instead of building them.

https://martinfowler.com/articles/injection.html

The Composition Root

Mark Seemann's write-up of the single-place-to-wire principle, including why pushing all construction to one location keeps the rest of the codebase free of the container.

https://blog.ploeh.dk/2011/07/28/CompositionRoot/

The Dependency Inversion Principle

The "D" in SOLID, the principle underneath every interface-first decision in this guide: high-level layers and low-level layers should both depend on abstractions, not on each other.

https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dependency_inversion_principle

That's the guide. You started with a four-line program and ended with a layered pipeline you could grow in three different directions without fear. The pattern scales down to this toy and up to real systems, because the idea underneath it, draw boundaries, depend on abstractions, assemble in one place, doesn't care how big the program gets.
